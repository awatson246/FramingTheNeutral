**Claude Sonnet**

> Legal certainty in the context of AI systems used in public decision-making refers to the
> principle that individuals and organizations must be able to understand, predict, and rely
> upon the legal rules governing automated decision-making processes that affect their
> rights, obligations, and entitlements. This principle has deep roots in the rule of law
> tradition and has been explicitly recognized in EU law, where the Court of Justice of the
> European Union has consistently held that legal certainty requires that rules be clear,
> precise, and predictable in their effects (see Case C-345/06, Gottfried Heinrich). When
> applied to AI systems in public administration, legal certainty requires several essential
> elements: First, it demands transparency about when and how AI systems are being used. The
> EU General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) Article 22 embodies this by requiring that
> individuals be informed of automated decision-making and have the right to obtain human
> intervention. This aligns with what scholars like Mireille Hildebrandt have termed
> 'technological due process' - the notion that procedural fairness must extend to
> algorithmic systems (Hildebrandt, 'Law as Information in the Era of Data-Driven Agency',
> 2016). Second, legal certainty requires that the legal basis for AI deployment be
> established through proper legislative processes rather than administrative discretion
> alone. The EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689) attempts to provide this framework by
> establishing risk-based categories and compliance requirements for AI systems,
> particularly high-risk systems used in public administration and access to essential
> services. Third, it necessitates the availability of meaningful remedies and review
> mechanisms. The Wisconsin Supreme Court's decision in State v. Loomis (881 N.W.2d 749,
> 2016) illustrates the tension here: the court allowed use of the proprietary COMPAS risk
> assessment tool in sentencing but required warnings about its limitations, acknowledging
> but not fully resolving concerns about opacity and contestability. Fourth, legal certainty
> demands that AI systems operate within ascertainable bounds - that their decision-making
> logic, even if complex, can be subjected to legal scrutiny. Article 5(1)(a) of the EU AI
> Act prohibits certain AI practices that manipulate behavior in ways that cause harm,
> reflecting the principle that AI systems must not operate in legally unaccountable ways.
> When legal certainty is absent in AI-driven public decision-making, several pathologies
> emerge: - Citizens cannot understand why they received particular treatment from
> government agencies, undermining their ability to challenge potentially erroneous or
> discriminatory decisions. This 'black box' problem has been documented extensively in
> contexts ranging from welfare benefits to immigration decisions. - Administrative law's
> traditional tools - such as requirements for reasoned decisions articulated in the US
> Administrative Procedure Act (5 U.S.C. § 551 et seq.) - become difficult or impossible to
> apply when the reasoning process is opaque or emergent. - Legal accountability fractures
> across multiple parties (algorithm developers, data providers, deploying agencies) without
> clear allocation of responsibility. The Council of Europe's Ad Hoc Committee on Artificial
> Intelligence (CAHAI) identified this 'responsibility gap' as a central challenge in its
> feasibility study for an AI legal framework (2020). - Fundamental rights guarantees become
> difficult to enforce. As the European Court of Human Rights suggested in Big Brother Watch
> v. UK (2021), technological systems that lack adequate safeguards and oversight may
> violate Article 8 (privacy) and Article 10 (freedom of expression) rights. - The principle
> of equality before the law is compromised when similarly situated individuals cannot
> determine whether they are being treated consistently, particularly when AI systems
> exhibit bias or drift over time, as documented in academic work by Barocas and Selbst
> ('Big Data's Disparate Impact', 2016). Legal certainty also requires temporal stability -
> individuals must be able to rely on the continuity of legal rules. With AI systems that
> learn and adapt, this creates novel challenges: when does a modification to an algorithm
> constitute a change in the legal rule itself? The EU AI Act addresses this partially by
> requiring quality management systems and post-market monitoring (Articles 17 and 72), but
> many questions remain unresolved. Ultimately, legal certainty in AI-driven public
> decision-making means that the law must remain intelligible and accessible to those it
> governs, even as decision-making mechanisms become technologically sophisticated. This may
> require new legal techniques - such as algorithmic impact assessments (proposed in various
> jurisdictions including Canada's Directive on Automated Decision-Making, 2019), mandatory
> transparency registers, or rights to explanation beyond those in GDPR Article 22. The
> absence of legal certainty creates what legal scholar Frank Pasquale has called 'black box
> society' conditions, where power operates without adequate legal constraint or public
> accountability.

> values. 4. **Individual Operators**: Humans in the loop who make final decisions aided by

> decision-making cannot be wholly delegated to algorithmic systems. **Accountable For

> existing laws including anti-discrimination law, data protection regulations (GDPR
> Articles 22, 35), consumer protection, and sector-specific regulations. 2. **Transparency

> logic, significance, and consequences. GDPR Article 13-15 establish information

> **Fairness and Non-Discrimination**: Accountability extends to ensuring AI systems do not
> perpetuate or amplify discrimination. The Algorithmic Accountability Act (proposed US
> legislation) would require impact assessments for automated decision systems affecting
> critical decisions about consumers. 4. **Safety and Robustness**: Developers are
> accountable for technical reliability, cybersecurity, and preventing foreseeable harms.

> Emerging frameworks recognize accountability for AI's broader societal effects, including
> environmental costs and labor impacts, as reflected in the UNESCO Recommendation's
> holistic approach. **Accountable To Whom:** 1. **Affected Individuals**: Those directly
> impacted by AI decisions should have access to explanations, challenges, and redress
> mechanisms. GDPR Article 22(3) provides rights to obtain human intervention and contest
> automated decisions. 2. **Regulatory Authorities**: Sectoral regulators, data protection
> authorities, and AI-specific oversight bodies (as proposed in the EU AI Act through
> national competent authorities and the European AI Board) enforce compliance and
> investigate violations. 3. **Civil Society and Public**: Democratic accountability
> requires transparency to enable public scrutiny. The Council of Europe's Framework
> Convention on AI and Human Rights emphasizes participatory governance and public
> accountability mechanisms. 4. **Investors and Stakeholders**: Corporate accountability
> frameworks extend to shareholders and stakeholders who may bear financial and reputational
> consequences of AI failures. **Mechanisms for Accountability:** 1. **Ex Ante Mechanisms**:
> Requirements for impact assessments (Data Protection Impact Assessments under GDPR Article

> Directive and proposed AI Liability Directive), tort law, administrative penalties (GDPR
